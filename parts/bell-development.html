Technical Development Log

February 10, 2026

Initial Prototyping

The making process of the bell started from a pleasurable metaphor: kids playing with sand moulds to turn sand into different shapes. We aimed to make a metal bell using industrial production methods (pressing sheet metal) but accessible for DIY. Could you press the top of a bell by just pulling a lever?

We started with 3D printed plastic moulds (PETG, gyroid infill) placed underneath a bearing press. The first test was promising—the PETG mould didn't indent—but the edges of the metal wrinkled. This initial design had only a slight curve, and a bell needs a more defined shape to function properly.

Exploring New Shapes and Moulds

Through several iterations, we tried multi-step forms and lubricated moulds, but wrinkling persisted. Experimenting with different shapes, such as a flower-shaped bell, redirected the buckling edges but still presented challenges. Even after milling an aluminium 'negative' mould, the edges kept wrinkling.

Metal Forming Advice

Consulting experts on sheet metal forming revealed that crash forming deep convex shapes is difficult due to pressure points and lack of material control. Techniques like deep drawing and rubber moulding are better suited but require immense pressure not available in a typical DIY workshop.

Furthermore, metal selection is crucial for acoustic properties. Harder alloys (like high carbon steel) produce better sound but are much harder to form with non-industrial equipment compared to softer metals like aluminium.

Alternative Instruments

Because pressing a traditional bell shape proved too demanding for DIY, we looked at alternative instruments with simpler geometry: pipe bells, triangles, and flutes. The pipe bell and triangle produce sounds similar to a bike bell and only require cutting a section of existing material.

Our first new prototype was a musical instrument with copper pipes supported at the nodal point. While sound volume depends heavily on mounting and the striker used, this direction simplifies the production process significantly, even if it shifts away from the original sand mould metaphor.

Conclusion

Moulding metal is difficult, but Design-for-DIY sometimes requires changing the product form itself to make it easier to build. The traditional shape of a bell also limits customization. In the future, we will continue testing different mounting solutions and striker materials to optimize the pipe bell approach.
