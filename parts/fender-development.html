Technical Development: Rolling the Fender

February 14, 2026

The fender's development started from a simple question: is it possible to make a mudguard by hand-cranking a DIY roller bender? My goal was to shape metal strips into functional fenders without high-tech industrial machinery.

Testing Plywood Rollers

To get a feel for the process, I turned custom profile rollers from laminated plywood on a lathe and tested them on a workshop machine using aluminum and steel sheets (0.5 to 2 mm). While the thinner pieces worked, the 2mm strips broke the plywood layers. I learned that plywood isn't strong enough, 2mm material is too difficult to form, and the metal tends to 'wander' without a guide.

DIY Machine & Buckling Challenges

I built a simple machine out of 2x4 lumber and 3D printed (PETG) rollers to see if hand power was sufficient, switching from a pyramid to a pinch-type configuration. I successfully rolled 0.5 – 1mm aluminum shapes, but encountered persistent 'wrinkling' along the edges.

This buckling happens because bending the metal into a convex cross-section stretches the top while compressing the edges. The material inevitably buckled where it was free to move.

Pivoting to Simpler Methods & Plastics

An expert noted that traditional metal fenders require tremendous force and special edge-folding tools to prevent buckling. I pivoted to trying the simplest possible shape: a flat, curved piece of aluminum. It was easy to make but splashed water to the sides, reducing its utility.

To fix this, I explored plastics. I prototyped a fender from a continuous strip of PETG thermoformed over a wooden mould and heat-welded the joints. Finally, I tested an "airplane wing" design: a steel rod frame wrapped in a shrinkable PVC battery sleeve. These experiments proved that while traditional metal rolling is tough in a DIY setting, alternative methods are just as promising and pleasurable to build!
